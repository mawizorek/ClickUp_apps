01 · What's under the wing

One utility quietly runs the whole valley.

The two hyperbolic cooling towers you spotted on the peninsula are Sequoyah Nuclear Plant, on Chickamauga Lake about 18 miles north of Chattanooga [1]. It's one node in a system most people never think about: the Tennessee Valley Authority.

TVA isn't a state grid and isn't a private company. It's a federally owned power corporation born out of the 1933 New Deal, today the largest public utility in the country [2]. It generates the power AND moves it, then sells wholesale to ~150 local utilities who bill your house. No shareholders. Surplus loops back into cheaper rates and infrastructure.

Nuclear's the big one, but gas is coming fast.

The mix is shifting right now

TVA's 2026 resource plan floats up to 26 GW of new natural gas by 2040, driven largely by AI data centers slamming the region with demand. Expect gas to climb and coal to keep shrinking [5].

03 · The twist

The same dams that lit up dirt-poor farms powered the bomb.

1933

Rock bottom, then FDR

The Tennessee Valley was one of the poorest, most flood-ravaged corners of America. Private utilities wouldn't wire the countryside; it wasn't profitable. So the government became the utility [2].

1942

Enter "Site X"

The Manhattan Project needed staggering electricity and empty land. TVA had both. The government dropped Oak Ridge, Tennessee in its backyard; the mile-long K-25 plant pioneered gaseous-diffusion uranium enrichment [6].

1959

Cutting the cord

TVA went self-funding. Zero taxpayer dollars since. It runs on power revenue and bonds, still owned by the U.S. government, still governed by a presidentially appointed board [2].

"Manhattan Project" is a codename, not a place. The bomb was really a Tennessee, New Mexico and Washington operation.
Named for the Army's Manhattan Engineer District · HQ'd in NYC on paper only

The work sprawled across three secret sites: Oak Ridge enriched uranium, Los Alamos designed the weapon, and Hanford made plutonium. Today all three form a single national park [7].

Two hard constraints drive the whole plan. Trinity opens just one day a year (3rd Sat of October, next Oct 17, 2026) [8], and Hanford's B Reactor tours are suspended for construction [9]. Los Alamos lab tours are lottery-only [10]. Lock those dates first, build everything else around them.

05 · Before you go

The nerd's pre-flight checklist.

  • Target the October Trinity date. Once a year, no reservation, but everything in New Mexico bends around it.
  • Enter the Los Alamos lottery early. Registration windows open months ahead; you're drawn at random.
  • Verify Hanford before booking flights. Reactor tours are paused for construction; don't assume.
  • Split it into two runs. Tennessee (Oak Ridge / Norris / the plants) is a weekend. New Mexico + Washington is the cross-country ambition.
  • Collect all three park units. Oak Ridge, Los Alamos and Hanford share one national park. Full set = bragging rights.
