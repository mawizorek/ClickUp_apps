The long way back

How we got here

1st c. BCE · Rome

Garum runs the empire

Romans fermented oily fish and guts under heavy salt in the sun until it broke down into a pungent liquid. They poured it on nearly everything: part ketchup, part soy sauce, part fish sauce. Salt made it possible, and salt is why it mattered.

Coastal Italy

Salt built the trade

Rome's salt came from the coastal pans (the salinae at Ostia), hauled inland along the Via Salaria, the "Salt Road." Salt didn't rot or decay, so it read as pure and permanent: sacred enough for offerings and covenants, valuable enough to anchor an economy.

Middle Ages

Garum nearly disappears

As Rome faded, so did large-scale garum. The recipe survived in pockets, kept alive quietly by fishing communities rather than empires.

Cetara · Amalfi Coast

The monks keep the flame

In the tiny fishing village of Cetara, anchovies caught in spring were salted, pressed, and left to mature in chestnut barrels. The amber liquid that dripped from the bottom became colatura di alici: garum's direct, refined descendant.

Today

Umami in a dropper

Aged for months (the finest, years, in oak), colatura is now protected as a heritage food. A few drops make everything taste more intensely of itself. You can buy the real thing and cook with 2,000 years of history tonight.

Anchovies · Salt · Time

How it's made

01

Catch & clean

Spring anchovies from the Gulf of Salerno, cleaned by hand within hours of the catch.

02

Salt & press

Layered with sea salt in chestnut-wood barrels (terzigni), weighted, and left under pressure.

03

Wait

Months of slow maturation. The salt draws water out by osmosis, so microbes can't spoil it: preservation and flavor in one move.

04

Drip & bottle

The amber liquid trickles from a hole in the barrel base, filtered clear. That drip is colatura.

Five ways to use it

What to cook

The flagship

Spaghetti alla Colatura

~15 min · no-cook sauce · serves 4
  • 400g spaghetti
  • 4 tbsp colatura
  • 8 tbsp olive oil
  • 1 garlic clove
  • chili
  • parsley
  1. Boil spaghetti in unsalted water. The colatura is salty enough.
  2. Gently warm the oil with a bashed garlic clove and chili. Don't brown the garlic, then pull it off the heat.
  3. Off the heat, stir the colatura into the warm oil. Never over direct flame: high heat wrecks it.
  4. Drain (save a splash of pasta water), toss into the oil, add water to emulsify into a glossy coat.
  5. Finish with chopped parsley.

This plate is a straight-line descendant of Roman garum. You're eating history.

Sea meets greens

Orecchiette, Clams & Broccoli

colatura ties it all together

Steam clams open, blanch the broccoli, then toss with orecchiette, garlic-chili oil, and a finishing drizzle of colatura. The sauce bridges the brine of the clams and the bitterness of the greens.

Eataly classic

Linguine & Broccoli Rabe

bitter greens + umami = Campania

Sauté broccoli rabe (erbe di campo) with garlic, chili flakes and good oil, fold through linguine, then season off-heat with colatura instead of salt. Rustic, fast, deeply savory.

Summer, cold

Shaved Fennel & Tomato Salad

colatura in the dressing

Shave fennel thin, add ripe tomatoes and salted capers, dress with olive oil and a few drops of colatura, then top with crispy panko. Light and refreshing with a salty umami hit: the perfect hot-weather plate.

The sneaky uses

A few drops, everywhere

its real value as a seasoning

Think of it as Worcestershire's classier Italian cousin. A little goes a long way, so treat it as a seasoning, not a sauce base:

  • into salad dressings
  • over roasted veg
  • over grilled fish
  • into braises
  • to deepen tomato sauce
